Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know before searching and filing your trademark in India.

A trademark search is not legally mandatory under the Trade Marks Act, 1999 — but it is strongly recommended before filing. Without a prior search, your application risks rejection by the Trade Marks Registry if a similar or identical mark already exists, causing delays, wasted government fees (₹4,500–₹9,000 per class), and potential legal disputes. A thorough search before filing significantly improves your chances of smooth registration.
An exact match search finds trademarks that are spelled identically or near-identically to your proposed name. A phonetic search (also called a "sounds-like" search) identifies marks that sound similar even if spelled differently — for example, "Kwikr" and "Quicker", or "Zomato" and "Zomatto". The Trade Marks Registry considers phonetically similar marks as potential conflicts, so running both types of search is essential before filing your application in India.
India follows the NICE Classification system, which divides all goods and services into 45 classes — Classes 1 to 34 cover physical goods, and Classes 35 to 45 cover services. Your trademark is only protected in the class(es) under which it is registered. For example, a clothing brand registers under Class 25, while a software company would register under Class 42. If you sell both products and services, you may need multiple class registrations. Trademarx lets you check availability across specific classes or all 45 at once.
Each trademark status in India has a specific meaning:

Registered — fully protected; the owner has exclusive rights.
Pending / Filed — submitted but not yet examined. May still become a conflict.
Objected — the Examiner has raised concerns; the applicant must respond.
Advertised — passed examination, published for a 4-month public opposition window.
Opposed — a third party has filed a challenge against registration.
Refused / Abandoned — the application failed.

Even Pending and Advertised marks can block your application — never ignore them in your search results.

For example, "Apple" in Class 31 (fresh fruits) could coexist with "Apple" in Class 9 (electronics), unless there is a likelihood of confusion about origin. However, well-known or famous trademarks enjoy trans-class protection and cannot be used or registered in any class by others. If an identical or similar mark exists in your class, you have several options:

1. Modify the name — add a distinctive word, change spelling, or pair it with a unique logo.
2. Check the mark's status — if it is Abandoned or Refused, you may be able to apply for the same name.
3. Conduct a prior-use investigation — if the existing mark has not been commercially used, a prior-use claim may be possible.
4. Negotiate a co-existence agreement with the existing owner.
5. Consider filing in a different class if your goods or services can be legitimately classified elsewhere.

The trademark registration timeline in India currently ranges from 18 to 36 months for a smooth application, and longer if objections or oppositions arise. The key stages are:

• Filing — Day 1, application number issued immediately.
• Examination — 6–12 months for the Registry to review.
• Response to objection (if raised) — 2–3 months to respond.
• Advertisement — published in the Trademark Journal after approval.
• Opposition period — 4 months for third parties to challenge.
• Registration certificate — issued after the opposition period closes.

Filing under the expedited route (at higher fees) can reduce examination time to approximately 1–3 months.

Purely descriptive or generic words — those that directly describe the goods or services — cannot be trademarked in India under Section 9 of the Trade Marks Act, 1999. For example, "Fresh" for a vegetable brand or "Fast" for a courier service would be refused. However, words that are suggestive, arbitrary, or fanciful can be registered — think "Swiggy" for food delivery or "Dunzo" for quick commerce. A word that has acquired distinctiveness through long and extensive commercial use can sometimes overcome this bar. Our experts can assess the registrability of your proposed name before you invest in filing.
To file a trademark application in India (Form TM-A), you will need:

1. Applicant details — full legal name and address (individual, LLP, or company).
2. The trademark — word, logo, or combination. Logos must be in JPG format, 8×8 cm.
3. Description of goods/services and the relevant trademark class(es).
4. Date of first use in commerce, if claiming prior use.
5. Power of Attorney (Form TM-48) if filing through an attorney — highly recommended.
6. MSME or Startup India certificate, if applicable, to claim the concessional fee of ₹4,500 instead of ₹9,000 per class.
